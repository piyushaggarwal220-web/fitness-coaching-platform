## Warmup Routine (Every Training Day — ~10 minutes)

Before you dive into your main lifts, spend about 10 minutes getting everything fired up. Start with 5 minutes of light cardio — treadmill walk at an incline, bike, or rower, whatever feels good. Then move into some joint mobility: arm circles, leg swings front-to-back and side-to-side, hip circles, bodyweight squats, and a few inchworms or world's greatest stretch. Keep it flowing, not static. Once you're warm, run through 2 lighter warm-up sets before your first heavy compound lift — something like empty bar x 10, then 50% of your working weight x 6. This primes the movement pattern without tiring you out.

## Training Intensity Guidance

On your big compound lifts — squats, deadlifts, rows, bench, overhead press — you should be leaving 1-2 reps in the tank. That means if you're doing 5 reps, you should feel like you could've done 6 or maybe 7 with good form. Don't grind out ugly reps on these movements. For accessory and isolation work, you can push closer to failure — especially on the last set, where 1-2 controlled "cheat reps" after you hit technical failure are fine. But never on squats, deadlifts, RDLs, lunges, or any heavy barbell compound. Those stay strict. If your form breaks, the set is over.

## Post-Workout Stretching Routine (End of Week or After Any Session)

Spend 10-15 minutes stretching the major muscle groups you've trained during the week. Hold each stretch for 30-60 seconds per side, breathe into it, don't bounce. Hit your quads (standing quad stretch or couch stretch), hamstrings (seated forward fold or single-leg stretch), hip flexors (kneeling lunge stretch), glutes (pigeon pose or figure-four stretch), chest (doorway pec stretch), lats (overhead side bend or hanging from a bar), and shoulders (cross-body arm stretch). If your lower back is tight, do some cat-cow and child's pose. Make this a ritual — it'll help with recovery and keep you moving well.

## Sleep Recommendations

You're already getting 7-8 hours, which is solid, but let's tighten it up. Aim for 7.5-9 hours consistently — athletic performance demands it, especially when you're balancing lifting and running. Try to get to bed by 10:30-11:00 pm if you're waking up around 7:00 am. Here are a few practical tips that'll work with your schedule:

- Cut off chai and coffee by 4:00 pm. Caffeine has a half-life of 5-6 hours, and that evening cup can mess with your deep sleep even if you don't feel it.
- Finish dinner by 8:30-9:00 pm when possible. You're eating at 9:00 pm now, which is fine, but if you can shift it 30 minutes earlier on some days, your digestion won't interfere with sleep quality.
- Dim the lights in your room after 9:00 pm. Bright overhead lights signal your brain to stay alert. Switch to lamps or warmer lighting.
- Keep your phone out of the bedroom, or at least face-down and on silent. If you use it as an alarm, get a cheap alarm clock. Doomscrolling before bed kills your sleep quality.
- Your room should be cool and dark. If you're in a warm climate, a fan or AC set to 22-24°C helps. Blackout curtains or an eye mask if streetlights are an issue.
- If your mind races at night, keep a small notebook by your bed. Do a "brain dump" before you sleep — write down anything you're thinking about for 5 minutes, then close it and let it go.
- Consider a short wind-down routine: 10 minutes of stretching or a few minutes of deep breathing (4 seconds in, 6 seconds out, repeat 10 times). Signals your nervous system that it's time to shut down.

You're doing great with the 2L of water — keep that up, but try to front-load it earlier in the day so you're not waking up to pee multiple times at night.
