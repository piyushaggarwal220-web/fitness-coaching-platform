## Warmup Routine (Every Training Day — ~10 minutes)

Before you touch a barbell, spend 10 minutes getting your body ready. Start with 5 minutes of light cardio — incline walk, bike, or rower, nothing crazy, just enough to get your heart rate up and break a light sweat. Then move into some joint mobility: arm circles, leg swings front to back and side to side, hip openers, thoracic rotations, and a few bodyweight squats. Finish with a couple of dynamic stretches — walking lunges, inchworms, or cat-cows. Once that's done, before you get into your main lifts, run through a couple of lighter warm-up sets — bar only, then 50%, then 70% of your working weight. This primes the movement pattern and keeps your joints happy.

## Day 1 — Lower Power

**Back Squat** — 4 sets x 5 reps  
This is your anchor. Control the descent, explode up. Leave 1-2 reps in the tank on each set — we're building strength, not testing your one-rep max today.

**Romanian Deadlift** — 3 sets x 6 reps  
Focus on the stretch in your hamstrings. Keep the bar close, hinge at the hips, don't round your back. Tempo should be controlled — 3 seconds down, 1 second up.

**Bulgarian Split Squat (Dumbbell)** — 3 sets x 8 reps per leg  
These are humbling but gold for single-leg strength and balance. Keep your torso upright, don't let your front knee cave in.

**Leg Press** — 3 sets x 12 reps  
Feet shoulder-width, push through your heels. Go deep but don't let your lower back round off the pad at the bottom.

**Standing Calf Raise (Machine)** — 3 sets x 15 reps  
Full range — all the way up, all the way down. Pause at the top for a second.

**Core Circuit:**  
- Hanging Leg Raises — 3 sets x 10 reps (or knee raises if full leg raises aren't there yet)  
- Plank — 3 sets x 45 seconds  
- Russian Twists (Dumbbell) — 3 sets x 20 reps total

## Post-Workout Stretching Routine (5-10 minutes)

After every training session, spend a few minutes stretching the major muscle groups you worked. Hold each stretch for 30-60 seconds per side, breathe deeply, don't bounce. Here's a simple flow:

- **Hamstrings:** Seated forward fold or standing toe touch  
- **Quads:** Standing quad stretch (pull your heel to your glute)  
- **Hip Flexors:** Kneeling lunge stretch  
- **Chest:** Doorway pec stretch or hands behind your back, open your chest  
- **Lats/Shoulders:** Overhead tricep stretch or cross-body shoulder stretch  
- **Calves:** Wall calf stretch, both straight and bent knee  

Don't skip this. Flexibility and mobility work now will save you from tightness and imbalances down the line.

## Sleep and Recovery

You're getting 7-8 hours, which is solid, but let's tighten it up. Aim for 7.5-9 hours consistently — that's where your body rebuilds muscle, regulates hormones, and manages stress. Since you're training in the evening, try to be in bed by 11 pm and up by 7 am. Here are some practical tweaks for better sleep:

- **Cut off chai/coffee by 4 pm.** Caffeine has a half-life of 5-6 hours, so that evening cup can wreck your sleep even if you don't feel wired.  
- **Dim the lights after 9 pm.** Bright overhead lights signal your brain it's still daytime. Switch to softer lamps or even a night mode on your phone.  
- **No screens 30-60 minutes before bed.** I know it's tough, but the blue light messes with melatonin production. Read a book, stretch, or just wind down.  
- **Keep your last meal at least 2 hours before bed.** You're eating dinner at 9 pm, so if you're in bed by 11, that's tight but workable. Just avoid heavy, greasy stuff late.  
- **Keep your room cool and dark.** If you've got a fan, use it. Blackout curtains or an eye mask help too.  
- **Consistent sleep schedule, even on weekends.** Your body loves routine. Try to keep your sleep and wake times within an hour of each other every day.  
- **If you're stressed or your mind is racing, try 5 minutes of deep breathing before bed.** In through your nose for 4 counts, hold for 4, out through your mouth for 6. Calms your nervous system.

Sleep is non-negotiable if you want to hit 62 kg and perform well. Treat it like part of your training.

## Training Intensity Guidance

Let's talk about how hard to push. On your big compound lifts — squats, deadlifts, bench, rows, overhead press — leave 1-2 reps in the tank. That means if you could do 7 reps, stop at 5 or 6. This keeps your form tight and your nervous system from getting fried. On isolation and accessory work — leg curls, curls, lateral raises, cable flies — you can push closer to failure, maybe even hit it on the last set. That's where you build volume and metabolic stress.

Now, here's the rule: you can use 1-2 controlled "cheat reps" on the last set of isolation exercises after you've hit strict failure. For example, if you're doing barbell curls and you can't get another clean rep, a little body english to squeeze out one more is fine. But — and this is critical — never on squats, deadlifts, RDLs, lunges, or any barbell compound movement. Those require perfect form every single rep. No exceptions. Cheating on a deadlift is how you blow out your back.

If a weight feels too heavy and your form is breaking down, drop it. Ego has no place in training. You're here to build, not to impress anyone.
