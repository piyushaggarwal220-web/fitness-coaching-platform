**WARMUP ROUTINE (10 minutes — do this before every session)**

Start with 2–3 minutes of light cardio (brisk walk on the treadmill or a few minutes on the bike). Then move into 5 minutes of joint mobility: arm circles, leg swings (forward/back and side-to-side), cat-cow stretches, bodyweight squats for ankle mobility, and some scapular circles with your arms. Finish with 2–3 minutes of light movement — maybe a few bodyweight squats, some arm circles, and a couple of light rows with just the bar to get your nervous system primed. Before you get into your main lifts, always run through a couple of lighter warm-up sets — nothing fancy, just enough to feel ready.

**DAY 1 — LOWER POWER (Monday)**

This is your main leg day. We're starting heavy on the squat because you love them, and honestly, they're the best bang for buck for fat loss. Keep the rep range low and controlled — leave a rep or two in the tank on the heavy sets.

**Main Lifts:**
- Barbell Back Squat: 4 sets x 5 reps (heavy focus; rest 2–3 min between sets)
- Romanian Deadlift (RDL): 3 sets x 6 reps (controlled tempo, 2-second descent)

**Accessory Work:**
- Leg Press: 3 sets x 8 reps (go a bit heavier here since it's machine-based)
- Walking Lunges (dumbbells): 3 sets x 10 reps per leg (tough but manageable)
- Leg Curl Machine: 2 sets x 12 reps (squeeze at the top, control the descent)

**Core Work:**
- Cable Wood Chops: 3 sets x 12 reps per side (anti-rotation strength)
- Hanging Knee Raises: 3 sets x 10 reps (or knee raises on a mat if hanging is tough; focus on control, not swinging)
- Dead Bug: 2 sets x 12 reps per side (slow and controlled)

**Daily Step Target: 8,000 steps** (add a 15-minute walk after the gym or during your lunch break)

**Coaching Note:** Squats are the star here — don't rush the depth. Go to parallel or just below, pause for a half-second, then drive through your heels. Your knees should track over your toes. If you feel like you're folding forward, drop the weight and rebuild the pattern.

**DAY 2 — UPPER PUSH (Tuesday)**

Focus on chest, shoulders, and triceps. This is where you build upper body pressing strength and muscle. Push isolation work closer to failure — you can afford an extra rep or two on the last set of chest press or shoulder work, but keep it controlled.

**Main Lifts:**
- Barbell Bench Press: 4 sets x 5 reps (chest-dominant; rest 2–3 min)
- Overhead Press (barbell): 3 sets x 6 reps (strict form, no leg drive)

**Accessory Work:**
- Dumbbell Incline Press: 3 sets x 8 reps (upper chest emphasis)
- Cable Flyes: 3 sets x 12 reps (squeeze and control; this is where you can push closer to failure)
- Tricep Rope Pushdown: 3 sets x 12 reps (last set can go to near-failure with 1–2 controlled cheat reps if needed)
- Lateral Raises (dumbbells): 2 sets x 15 reps (light weight, high reps for shoulder health)

**Core Work:**
- Weighted Ab Crunch (cable): 3 sets x 12 reps (controlled tempo)
- Pallof Press: 3 sets x 10 reps per side (anti-rotation, same as wood chops but different pattern)
- Plank Hold: 3 sets x 40–50 seconds (isometric strength)

**Daily Step Target: 7,000 steps** (lighter day; just your normal office movement)

**Coaching Note:** On bench press, keep your shoulder blades retracted — think about pulling the bar down to your chest rather than just pushing it away. Elbows should be at about 45 degrees from your body, not flared out wide. Control the descent; don't let it crash down.

Rows are your main event here. This is your chance to build a strong back, which will improve your posture (especially as an office worker) and balance out all the pressing from Tuesday.

**Main Lifts:**

**Core Work:**
- Ab Wheel Rollout: 3 sets x 8 reps (or knee-based if standing is too tough; this is anti-extension strength)

**Daily Step Target: 8,000 steps** (similar to Monday; add a walk after the gym)

**Coaching Note:** On bent-over rows, keep your chest up and core tight. Pull the bar to your lower chest or upper abdomen — not your hips. Your elbows should stay close to your body. If your lower back is rounding, drop the weight; form over ego every time.

**TRAINING INTENSITY GUIDELINES**

Here's how to think about effort across the week: on your heavy compound lifts (squats, deadlifts, bench, rows, overhead press), you should leave a rep or two in the tank — aim for RPE 7–8 out of 10. This means if you could do 6 reps, stop at 5. This is safer for a beginner and actually drives better long-term progress because you can recover faster and stay consistent.

On accessory and isolation work (leg press, flyes, curls, leg extensions, etc.), you can push harder — RPE 8–9, meaning you're closer to failure but not quite there. On the very last set of an isolation exercise, you can go to actual failure or allow a couple of controlled "cheat reps" (like a slight swing on a barbell curl or a tiny knee bend on a leg extension) only after you've hit strict failure. Never do this on compound movements like squats, deadlifts, RDLs, lunges, or barbell presses — those need perfect form every rep.

If a weight feels unstable or your form is breaking down, drop it. You're building a foundation here, not proving anything.

**COOLDOWN & STRETCHING (5–10 minutes after each session)**

After you finish, spend a few minutes stretching the major muscles you just worked. Hold each stretch for 30–60 seconds, and breathe deeply.

- **Lower Body Days:** quad stretch (30 sec per leg), hamstring stretch (30 sec per leg), glute stretch (pigeon pose or figure-4, 30 sec per side), calf stretch (30 sec per leg), hip flexor lunge stretch (30 sec per side)
- **Upper Body Days:** chest stretch (doorway or across-body, 30 sec per side), lat stretch (reach overhead and lean, 30 sec per side), shoulder stretch (arm across body, 30 sec per side), tricep stretch (reach behind head, 30 sec per side)

Don't rush this. Stretching isn't just about flexibility — it's about bringing your nervous system down from high intensity and priming your body for recovery.

**SLEEP & RECOVERY**

You're already doing great with 7–8 hours, but let's dial it in for fat loss and recovery. Aim for **7.5–9 hours per night**, ideally going to bed around **11:00 PM–11:30 PM** and waking around **6:30 AM–7:00 AM**. This syncs well with your office schedule and gives your body enough time to repair and adapt.

Here are some practical sleep hacks suited to your lifestyle:

1. **Chai cutoff:** Stop drinking tea or chai after **4:00 PM**. Your evening chai at 6:30 PM is fine for now (you love it), but anything after dinner should be herbal or decaf. Caffeine lingers for 6–8 hours and will mess with your sleep quality.

2. **Screen time:** Put your phone away **30–45 minutes before bed**. The blue light from screens suppresses melatonin, which is your sleep hormone. Use that time to read, stretch, or just wind down.

3. **Meal timing:** Your 9:00 PM dinner is a bit late for sleep — ideally, finish eating by **8:30 PM** so your digestion isn't in overdrive when you're trying to sleep. If you're hungry later, a small glass of warm milk or a banana is fine.

4. **Room temperature:** Keep your bedroom cool (around **18–20°C** if possible) and dark. This is when your body naturally cools down for sleep.

5. **Stress management:** You mentioned moderate stress — try 5 minutes of deep breathing or box breathing (4 counts in, 4 counts hold, 4 counts out, 4 counts hold) before bed. This signals to your nervous system that it's time to rest.

6. **Water timing:** You're drinking 2L daily, which is good. Finish most of it by **7:00 PM** so you're not waking up multiple times to pee. Hydrate during the day and at the gym, not right before bed.

7. **Consistency:** Go to bed and wake up at the same time every day, even on weekends. Your body loves routine, and this will make fat loss easier because your hormones stay stable.
