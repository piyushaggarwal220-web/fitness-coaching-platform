**Warmup Routine (10 minutes before every session)**

Start with 5 minutes of light cardio — treadmill walk at a brisk pace, elliptical, or stationary bike, whatever's available. Then move into some joint mobility: 10 arm circles each direction, 10 leg swings per leg (front-to-back and side-to-side), 10 bodyweight squats, 10 walking lunges, and a few cat-cow stretches if your lower back feels tight. Before you get into your main lifts, run through a couple of lighter warm-up sets — one set at 50% of your working weight, one at 75%, just to get the movement pattern dialed in and your joints ready.

**Post-Workout Stretching Routine (5-10 minutes after every training session)**

After your last set, spend a few minutes stretching the muscles you just worked. Hold each stretch for 30-60 seconds per side, no bouncing. For lower body days: quad stretch (standing or lying), hamstring stretch (seated or standing), hip flexor stretch (lunge position), calf stretch (against a wall). For upper body days: doorway chest stretch, overhead tricep stretch, cross-body shoulder stretch, seated spinal twist. This helps with recovery and keeps you mobile, especially important with all the travel and sitting.

**Training Intensity Guidance**

For the big compound lifts — squats, deadlifts, RDLs, bench press, rows — you should be leaving 1-2 reps in the tank on every set. That means if the set calls for 5 reps, you should feel like you could do 6 or maybe 7 if someone had a gun to your head, but you stop at 5. This keeps you safe, keeps your form clean, and still builds strength. On isolation and accessory work — leg curls, bicep curls, lateral raises, that kind of stuff — you can push closer to failure, maybe leaving just 1 rep in reserve. On the last set of isolation exercises, if you hit true failure and want to squeeze out 1-2 controlled cheat reps (a little body english, a bit of momentum), that's fine — but only on isolation moves, and only after you've hit failure with strict form. Never cheat on squats, deadlifts, RDLs, lunges, or any barbell compound movement. Those need to stay clean.

**Sleep Recommendations**

You're getting 7-8 hours now, which is solid, but let's aim for 7.5-9 hours consistently, especially during this recomposition phase — sleep is when your body repairs muscle and regulates hunger hormones. Based on your schedule, if you're eating dinner around 9 PM, try to be winding down by 10:30 PM and in bed by 11 PM, aiming to wake up around 7-7:30 AM. A few practical tips: cut off chai and coffee by 4 PM (I know that's tough, but caffeine has a long half-life), keep your phone out of the bedroom or at least on silent and face-down, have your last meal at least 2 hours before bed so digestion doesn't mess with your sleep, keep the room cool and dark (blackout curtains or an eye mask help), try some deep breathing or a short meditation before bed if work stress is high (even 5 minutes helps), and if you're traveling across time zones, try to adjust your sleep schedule a day or two before you leave. Consistent sleep timing matters as much as duration, so even on weekends or rest days, try not to swing more than an hour from your usual wake time.
